FREE VIBRATION OF CANTILEVER BEAM

A cantilever beam is a beam that is fixed at one end and free at the other end. When a cantilever beam is subjected to a forced vibration, it will oscillate at a frequency that is determined by the forcing frequency and the natural frequency of the beam.
The equation of motion for a forced vibration of a cantilever beam is given by:
EIy'''' + cy''' + ky = F(x, t)
where:
EI is the flexural rigidity of the beam
y is the transverse displacement of the beam
c is the damping coefficient
k is the stiffness coefficient
F(x,t) is the forcing function

The forcing function can be any type of function, but it is typically represented by a harmonic function of the form:
F(x, t) = F_0 sin(ωt + φ)
where:
F_0 is the amplitude of the forcing function
ω is the angular frequency of the forcing function
φ is the phase angle of the forcing function
The solution to the equation of motion for a forced vibration of a cantilever beam is given by:
y(x, t) = Y(x) sin(ωt + φ - α)
where:
Y(x) is the amplitude of the steady-state response of the beam
α is the phase angle of the steady-state response of the beam
The amplitude and phase angle of the steady-state response can be determined by solving the following equation:
(EIy'''' + cy''' + ky) - F_0 sin(ωt + φ) = 0

The steady-state response of the beam will be resonant when the forcing frequency is equal to the natural frequency of the beam. At resonance, the amplitude of the steady-state response will be very large, and the beam may even fail.
Examples of forced vibration of cantilever beams

Some examples of forced vibration of cantilever beams include:

A cantilever beam that is subjected to a rotating unbalance
A cantilever beam that is subjected to a harmonic excitation from a machine
A cantilever beam that is subjected to a wind load
